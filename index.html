How do aid and remittances shape the wellbeing of island nations?

FAR-SIDS is a two year study across Jamaica, Tonga, and Comoros that pairs econometric modelling with community led fieldwork to understand dependency, agency, and self-reliance in Small Island Developing States.

The question

Small island nations receive some of the world's largest flows of aid and remittances relative to their size. What do those flows actually leave behind?

FAR-SIDS traces how foreign aid and money sent home by relatives abroad translate into real wellbeing on the ground, and where they strengthen, or quietly weaken, a community's own capacity to thrive.

The project deliberately begins from island agency and self-determination rather than from vulnerability. The people who live these economies are treated as the experts on them.

It studies three nations in three different oceans, each with a distinct history of migration, aid, and resilience, so that what is learned in one place can be read against the others.

The work is hosted at the Institute for Islands and Small States at the University of Malta, itself a small island state, and runs with partner universities in each country studied.

How

Two strands, woven together

Numbers tell you what changed. People tell you why it mattered. FAR-SIDS needs both, in that order.

Strand 01 · Measuring

Econometrics

Time series models (the ARDL approach) trace how aid and remittance flows move with wellbeing indicators in each country, drawing on World Bank and OECD data reaching back to 1980. The result is a long view of cause and effect, country by country.

Strand 02 · Listening

Ethnography

Interviews, focus groups, and household surveys of 250 to 300 families per country put the lived experience of aid and remittances at the centre. Listening comes first, so that the survey asks the questions communities themselves raise.

Framework. The work is read through the Sustainable Livelihoods Framework and grounded in three principles carried from earlier research with Pacific communities: relationship, reciprocity, and respect. Findings are returned to the communities who shaped them.
