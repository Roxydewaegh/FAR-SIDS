How do aid and remittances shape the wellbeing of island nations?

FAR-SIDS is a two year study across Jamaica, Tonga, and Comoros that pairs econometric modelling with community led fieldwork to understand dependency, agency, and self-reliance in Small Island Developing States.

THIS WEEK ON THE PROJECT

 

The question

Why it matters

Small island nations receive some of the world's largest flows of aid and remittances relative to their size. What do those flows actually leave behind?

FAR-SIDS traces how foreign aid and money sent home by relatives abroad translate into real wellbeing on the ground, and where they strengthen, or quietly weaken, a community's own capacity to thrive.

The project deliberately begins from island agency and self-determination rather than from vulnerability. Island communities are treated as the experts on their own lives and economies.

It studies three nations in three different oceans, each with a distinct history of migration, aid, and resilience, so that what is learned in one place can be read against the others.

The work is hosted at the Islands and Small States Institute at the University of Malta, itself a small island state, and runs with partner universities in each country studied.

How

Measuring and listening, woven together

Numbers tell you what changed. People tell you why it mattered. FAR-SIDS needs both, and neither makes sense without the other.

Measuring

Econometrics

Time series models (the ARDL approach) trace how aid and remittance flows move with wellbeing indicators in each country, drawing on World Bank and OECD data reaching back to 1980. The result is a long view of cause and effect, country by country.

Listening

Ethnography

Interviews, focus groups, and household surveys of 250 to 300 families per country put the lived experience of aid and remittances at the centre, so that the numbers are read alongside the meaning communities give them.

Framework. The work is read through the Sustainable Livelihoods Framework and grounded in three principles carried from earlier research with Pacific communities: relationship, reciprocity, and respect. Findings are returned to the communities who shaped them.

The literature review

Reading the field before entering it

The literature review is not a numbered deliverable, but it is the foundation the numbered ones stand on. It runs through everything: the variable selection for the econometrics, the household survey going into the ethics package, the seminars, and the papers. Along the way it throws off more focused pieces of work, and the first of those are already under way.

The question. How do foreign aid and remittances relate to wellbeing and livelihoods in Small Island Developing States, and where are the gaps?
The first paper

Mapping the evidence

A PRISMA systematic review, the project's first paper, mapping what has been studied on aid, remittances, and wellbeing in SIDS, which methods and models were used, and which variables they drew on. It feeds the econometric variable list and is bound for DevNet 2026 in Wellington, with the abstract due at the end of August.

Toward the field

Designing the instrument

Reading on household survey design for remittances and livelihoods, drawing on the World Bank LSMS materials and earlier coastal communities research. This is the review turned toward practice: it sharpens the survey instrument before drafting, ahead of the ethics submission.

All of this is the project's public engagement plan in action. In March 2027 FAR-SIDS opens its social media channels under the banner Island Voices, Global Impact, a formal project milestone: the accounts go live, the first posts are drawn from what this room has already made, and engagement is tracked from that day on. The channels launch with a full room behind them, not a cold start.

Outreach · The listening post

Listening before speaking

Before the project can say anything worth hearing, it listens. These are running threads of what island communities and their diasporas say in public about aid and money from away, one thread per island, growing as the project listens. Themes are paraphrased rather than quoted, private posts are never reproduced, and nothing here is research data.

Outreach · Visualising stories

Research storytelling

Academic research rarely leaves academia in a form people can use. Each entry here takes one study or report on aid, remittances, and wellbeing and turns its central finding into a visual story, a chart, a map, or an illustrated figure that speaks for itself. The opening set is one story for each island and one for Small Island Developing States as a whole, and the collection grows from there.

Outreach · Island voices

The islands speak for themselves

This page belongs to the people the project works with. During fieldwork in Jamaica, Tonga, and Comoros, participants who choose to, and who give their consent, can share their stories here in whatever form they wish: photographs, artwork, video, or their own words. Over time it becomes a living platform where the islands tell their own story of aid, remittances, and daily life. Until then the page stays quiet, on purpose.
