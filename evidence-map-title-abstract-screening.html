FAR-SIDS systematic review, a thematic map of the included studies

The FAR-SIDS systematic review asks what is known about how foreign aid and remittances support livelihoods and wellbeing in small island developing states. This page maps the literature gathered so far. Of the 1,133 records screened, 282 studies came through the first stage, each one labelled by hand with the themes discussed in its abstract, and those labels, grouped into eight broader themes, are what the islands below show.

Click an island to open its theme, a sub-theme to see its labels, and a label to see the studies behind it. Island size reflects the number of studies touching the theme, and a study can appear on more than one island. The two flows overlap at the centre, since the studies engaging both are the ground FAR-SIDS builds on. How the map was made, with the files behind it, is explained at the bottom of the page.

Case study locations and methods

These two groups are not thematic categories like the eight above, but they hold equally important information. The location and methods labels were set aside during the thematic coding and grouped separately, to record where the studies were conducted and how they were carried out. The regional spread shows the literature leaning heavily towards the Pacific, with less on the Caribbean and very little on the Indian Ocean islands, the least studied region and the one where Comoros sits. The methods labels show a field dominated by macroeconomic and econometric analysis, with far fewer studies working at household level or with qualitative material, which is where the FAR-SIDS mixed methods design can add something. The methods group also holds the conceptual, theoretical and review contributions. A few bookkeeping tags from screening (empirical, background, methods) were filed with the sub-group that fits the studies carrying them; they mark a handful of records only and say nothing about the rest. Study design for every included study will be recorded systematically at the data extraction stage set out in the protocol.

How this map was made

The records came from the database searches set out in the review protocol. Of the 1,690 records identified across the four databases, 557 duplicates were removed, 536 with TERA, a free deduplication tool chosen because Rayyan, the screening platform that holds the records for the PRISMA protocol, charges a fee for that step at scale, and 21 more by Rayyan's own duplicate detection. The remaining 1,133 records then went through title and abstract screening in Rayyan, the first screening stage of the protocol, where each record was read and judged against the inclusion criteriaA study was included if it is: empirical, or a strong conceptual contribution, on foreign aid or remittances or both; reporting a wellbeing or livelihood outcome, applied broadly (studies with only macroeconomic outcomes join a separate macro stratum); set in a small island developing state, or a small economy whose findings transfer; peer reviewed, or substantial grey literature with clear methods or data; written in English, French, or Spanish; and published from 2006 onward. The full criteria, with their calibration record, are in section 3 of the review protocol.. 282 studies came through, and during that reading every included article was labelled by hand with the themes discussed in its abstract, 760 distinct labels in all.

Those labels were then revised and grouped into broader thematic categories, the same process as thematic coding in qualitative research. Eight thematic categories emerged: aid and donor politics; remittances and diaspora; migration and labour mobility; livelihoods and wellbeing; economy, growth and finance; climate change, disasters and resilience; culture, society and agency; and governance and institutions. Each distinct label was filed under one category and one sub-theme, and labels describing an outcome were filed under that outcome, so a label like impact of remittances on educational outcomes appears under education. Because such compound labels name a flow, their studies are still counted within aid and donor politics or remittances and diaspora as the label requires, and both categories list the studies counted on this basis, so the two flow categories cover every study whose labels mention aid or remittances. The location and methods labels were set aside into the two groups above. The original labels stay visible under each category so every grouping decision can be checked against what was written during screening; obvious typos were corrected and a few long labels that the export had broken apart at commas were put back together.

Two files sit behind this page. The Rayyan export holds every record with its labels in the first column, exactly as written during screening, typos and all; article abstracts are omitted from the online copy for copyright reasons.
